Cellular Automaton

A cellular automaton is simply a grid made of cells that have two states: on or off. Given a simple set of rules, these cells can turn into relatively complex systems allowing us to simulate
physics, computation, and biology. The most astonishing thing about cellular automaton is that certain sets of rules can be Turing-complete.

Cellular automaton can be divided into four classifications:

1. Automata in which patterns generally stabilize into homogeneity.
2. Automata in which patterns evolve into mostly stable or oscillating structure.
3. Automate in which patterns evolve in a seemingly chaotic fashion.
4. Automata in which patterns become extremely complex and can last a long time.
The second class can be seen above, in a simulation of Conway’s Game of Life. The fourth class is thought to be computationally universal.

Conway's Game of Life

The above simulation is Conway’s Game of Life. It is a one-player game, meaning its results are entirely dependent on its starting state, and consists of the following rules:

1. Any live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies, as if by underpopulation.
2. Any live cell with two or three live neighbors lives on to the next generation.
3. Any live cell with more than three live neighbors dies, as if by overpopulation.
4. Any dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes a live cell, as if by reproduction.
It starts off with each cell in a random position, either 1 (black) or 0 (white). From there, it follows the four rules in order to create the next generation. It looks as though
a random system becomes a system with static and oscillating objects.

There are oscillating objects with periods of 2 (the most common) to 15 (the least common). There are also six types of gliders. These gliders are just oscillations that are of a shape
capable of moving from one side of the board to another while still maintaining its pattern.

Biology

Cellular automaton can be observed in real life in the patterns of certain seashells. It can be argued, however, that our entire lives may just be a really complicated cellular automaton. If
we can find a set of rules that dictate our lives and surroundings, we can easily (with a large enough computer) simulate our whole world.

In fact, our own world can be viewed as Turing-complete since we have the ability to create Turing machines. With this in mind, we could classify our universe as the fourth classification of
cellular automaton.

Greater Philosophical Implications

Like most math, the cellular automaton has grand implications for our lives. Cellular automaton can lead to complex behavior derived from a seemingly random start. Given enough space
(in the form of cells) and enough time, a cellular automaton can create shapes, patterns, and feedback loops.

Theoretically, if we have a computer capable of simulating a cellular automaton with even a few basic rules, and allow it to run for thousands of years with an ever-expanding
amount of cells, the possibilities are endless. Of course, this is all theoretical so take it with a rather large pinch of salt. Yet, the similarities between systems of chaos that propagate into stable
oscillations (such as the planets orbit around the Sun) in our own world and in the world of cellular automaton is extremely uncanny.
